## **Executive Summary**

The paradigm of artificial intelligence is currently undergoing a profound architectural shift, migrating from centralized, cloud-bound infrastructural models toward decentralized, localized edge computing. This transition necessitates novel system architectures capable of processing continuous, unbounded streams of unstructured sensory data within the strict confines of localized hardware. The present research investigates the theoretical frameworks, empirical bottlenecks, and systems engineering strategies required to establish a continuous "sensory perimeter" on resource-constrained central processing units (CPUs). This sensory perimeter—conceptually analogous to the biological "skin" operating independently from the executive reasoning "nucleus" of a cognitive architecture—relies on the integration of Hierarchical Temporal Memory (HTM) spatial poolers, heavily quantized small-parameter language models (SLMs), and dynamic topological graph databases governed by Hebbian learning principles.

An exhaustive analysis of the academic consensus reveals that HTM spatial poolers provide an exceptionally robust, unsupervised baseline for parsing unstructured byte streams and textual data. By utilizing competitive Hebbian learning and homeostatic excitability control, HTM algorithms successfully convert noisy, high-dimensional input streams into stable Sparse Distributed Representations (SDRs).1 This topological preservation occurs without the computational overhead of backpropagation, making it highly suitable for continuous operation. However, the extraction of complex semantic relationships from unstructured text frequently requires the zero-shot abstraction capabilities of transformer-based architectures. Consequently, models in the 1-billion to 3-billion parameter range (e.g., Llama 3.2 1B/3B, Microsoft Phi-3.1 Mini), quantized into highly efficient formats like GGUF and executed via C++ frameworks such as llama.cpp, are deployed as transient sensory filters.3

Despite software optimizations, empirical benchmarks demonstrate that continuous SLM inference on localized CPUs is severely bottlenecked by memory bandwidth and thermal dynamics. The autoregressive nature of token generation forces the continuous shuttling of model weights across the memory bus, rapidly saturating physical bandwidth and exhausting the silicon's thermal budget.3 The resulting thermal throttling leads to catastrophic latency spikes and computational jitter, which fundamentally threaten the viability of an unbroken sensory perimeter.6 Addressing these physical limitations requires rigorous systems engineering, specifically the implementation of "metabolic capping" and aggressive CPU core-pinning to isolate the sensory inference loop from the operating system's default scheduling behaviors.8

Finally, the transient outputs of these sensory models must be captured and structured to form long-term knowledge. Translating unstructured data into dynamic topological graph databases provides a scalable solution for localized intelligence.10 By applying biologically inspired Hebbian learning algorithms ("fire together, wire together") to the graph's topology, nodes and edges representing semantic entities are continuously strengthened through co-activation or pruned through disuse.11 While prominent academic counter-arguments warn of the mathematical instability inherent in pure Hebbian systems and advocate for strict homeostatic or gradient-based regularizations 13, the synthesis of these mechanisms provides a viable blueprint for an autonomous, self-regulating sensory AI architecture capable of operating perpetually on edge hardware.

## **Theoretical Efficacy of Spatial Pooling and Hebbian Learning for Unstructured Text**

### **The Neocortical Paradigm of Hierarchical Temporal Memory**

Hierarchical Temporal Memory (HTM) is a biologically constrained machine learning algorithm that directly models the structural and algorithmic properties of the mammalian neocortex.2 Unlike traditional deep neural networks, which rely on dense tensor multiplications, global error backpropagation, and extensive offline training epochs, HTM is designed for continuous, online, unsupervised learning.16 The foundational component of the HTM framework is the Spatial Pooler (SP), which is responsible for continuously encoding streams of sensory inputs into Sparse Distributed Representations (SDRs).18

The theoretical efficacy of the Spatial Pooler lies in its capacity to preserve the topology of the input space while simultaneously reducing dimensionality and enforcing sparsity. In an HTM network, input patterns that share a large number of co-active neurons—indicating spatial or semantic similarity—are grouped together into a common output representation.18 The SP maintains a fixed level of output sparsity, typically activating only a minute fraction (e.g., 2%) of available mini-columns within a defined local inhibition radius.19 This strict enforcement of sparsity ensures that the resulting representations are highly robust to noise, resistant to catastrophic forgetting, and resilient to the degradation of individual network nodes, making the algorithm exceptionally well-suited for processing erratic, unstructured data streams in real-time.2

In the context of processing unstructured text or raw byte streams, the application of HTM requires specialized encoding mechanisms to translate symbolic data into binary arrays. Academic literature demonstrates that semantic and syntactic structures can be effectively parsed using Unicode-based word-encoding methods.21 In empirical studies focusing on the extraction of complex geoscientific dataset names from unstructured academic literature, researchers successfully encoded thousands of text strings into binary vectors, allowing the HTM model to learn the statistical structure of the text sequences.21 The Spatial Pooler processes these encoded text streams and generates predictive vectors. Subsequent classification mechanisms compute the similarity between the SP's predictive vector and the actual subsequent word encoder; if the similarity exceeds a specified threshold, the data is recognized as a coherent semantic entity.21 This mechanism bypasses the rigid limitations of deterministic parsing methods, such as hardcoded regular expressions, enabling the sensory perimeter to dynamically adapt to novel terminologies and syntactic variations.

### **Hebbian Learning Mechanics and Topological Preservation**

The primary learning mechanism driving the adaptation of the HTM Spatial Pooler is competitive Hebbian learning. Rooted in Donald Hebb's 1949 postulate—often summarized as "neurons that fire together, wire together"—Hebbian plasticity dictates that the synaptic connection between two neurons is strengthened when they exhibit synchronous activation.22 Within the mathematical formalization of the SP algorithm, this biological principle is translated into a highly efficient local update rule governing synaptic "permanence" values.24

During the processing of a continuous data stream, each SP mini-column evaluates its overlap with the active input bits. A local inhibition mechanism enforces a winner-take-all competition, ensuring that only the mini-columns with the highest overlap scores within a specific radius become active.2 Once the active mini-columns are selected, the Hebbian learning rule is applied: the permanence values of synaptic connections to active input bits are incremented (analogous to Long-Term Potentiation, or LTP), while the connections to inactive input bits are decremented (analogous to Long-Term Depression, or LTD).25 This localized update rule allows the Spatial Pooler to continuously adapt to the changing statistics of the input stream without requiring global optimization algorithms.18

To prevent a small subset of highly active mini-columns from dominating the network and to ensure the efficient utilization of all available computational resources, the SP incorporates a homeostatic excitability control known as "boosting".2 The boosting mechanism dynamically tracks the historical activation frequency of each mini-column. If a mini-column's activation rate falls below a target threshold, its overlap score is artificially multiplied by a boost factor, increasing its probability of winning subsequent competitive learning phases.2 This homeostatic regulation ensures that the network maintains high entropy and that the learned topological structures accurately reflect the diverse statistical distribution of the unstructured text or byte streams encountered by the sensory perimeter.

## **Empirical Bottlenecks of CPU-Bound, Quantized Sensory Models**

While the HTM Spatial Pooler provides a highly efficient, unsupervised mechanism for structural pattern recognition and anomaly detection, the extraction of high-level semantic abstractions from a sensory stream often necessitates the zero-shot and few-shot reasoning capabilities of transformer-based language models. To function effectively as a localized sensory perimeter, these models must operate strictly within the hardware constraints of the host system, precluding reliance on distributed cloud inference or dedicated data-center GPUs. Consequently, the architecture relies on Small Language Models (SLMs) in the 1-billion to 3-billion parameter range (e.g., Llama 3.2 1B/3B, Qwen2 1.5B, Microsoft Phi-3.1 Mini), heavily quantized and executed via CPU-optimized frameworks such as llama.cpp.3

### **The Memory Bandwidth Wall and Compute Constraints**

The deployment of SLMs for continuous active inference on edge CPUs exposes severe hardware bottlenecks, primarily governed by the dichotomy between compute availability and memory bandwidth. The execution of a transformer model involves two distinct phases: the prefill stage (processing the input prompt or sensory context) and the decoding stage (generating the subsequent tokens).28

The prefill stage is fundamentally compute-bound. The CPU must process the entire sequence of the input stream through dense matrix multiplications to populate the Key-Value (KV) cache.28 During this phase, performance is dictated by the CPU's core count, clock frequency, and the availability of advanced vector extensions (such as AVX2 or AVX-512).3 Conversely, the decoding stage is an autoregressive process that is severely memory-bandwidth bound. For every single token generated during inference, the entirety of the model's parameters must be transferred from the system's main Random Access Memory (RAM) through the memory bus, into the CPU's L2 and L3 caches, and finally to the arithmetic logic units (ALUs).3

Standard consumer and edge computing devices typically utilize DDR5 or LPDDR5x RAM, which provides a maximum theoretical bandwidth ranging from 60 GB/s to 120 GB/s.30 This physical limitation creates a hard ceiling on inference throughput, commonly measured in tokens per second (tk/s). To circumvent the "memory bandwidth wall," frameworks like llama.cpp rely heavily on post-training quantization. By converting high-precision floating-point weights (FP16) into 4-bit or 8-bit integer formats (e.g., the GGUF Q4\_0 or Q4\_K\_M formats), the physical size of the model is drastically reduced.27 A 3-billion parameter model quantized to 4-bits occupies less than 2 gigabytes of memory, proportionally reducing the data payload that must cross the memory bus per token and thereby multiplying the theoretical token generation rate.27 Extensive empirical evaluations indicate that modern SLMs quantized to 4-bit integer representations retain upwards of 99% of their baseline reasoning accuracy on academic benchmarks, confirming their viability as precise sensory filters.32

### **Thermal Dynamics and Latency Spikes**

A critical vulnerability of deploying a continuous sensory perimeter on localized hardware is the rapid accumulation of heat and the subsequent degradation of system performance. In standard user-facing applications, LLM inference occurs in discrete bursts followed by long periods of idle time, allowing the silicon to cool. However, an autonomous sensory "skin" requires the SLM to evaluate ambient data streams perpetually.

When subjected to a continuous inference workload via llama.cpp, the CPU remains at maximum utilization. Modern processor architectures—including ARM-based Snapdragon X Elites, Apple M-series silicon, and Intel Core series—rely heavily on dynamic frequency scaling (turbo boosting).6 The CPU aggressively increases its clock multiplier to maximize performance until it hits a predefined thermal threshold (Thermal Design Power, or TDP).5 Once this thermal budget is exhausted, the operating system's Advanced Configuration and Power Interface (ACPI) governor forcefully throttles the clock speeds to prevent physical degradation of the silicon.5

Empirical benchmarks of continuous SLM inference on consumer edge devices reveal catastrophic performance degradation during sustained thermal throttling. For instance, testing a 10-core Snapdragon X processor with llama.cpp utilizing all threads resulted in rapid thermal saturation, causing the token generation rate to plummet by 40% from its peak performance.6 This thermal collapse directly manifests as severe latency spikes, rendering the Time-to-First-Token (TTFT) and Inter-Token Latency (ITL) highly erratic and unpredictable.4 Furthermore, continuous memory fetching induces severe cache thrashing; the SLM weights displace other critical operating system processes from the L2/L3 caches, leading to frequent page faults, context switching overhead, and temporary system lockups.27 Without intervention, the sensory perimeter effectively cannibalizes the compute resources required by the system's executive reasoning layers.

## **Architectural Solutions for CPU Resource Segregation and "Metabolic Capping"**

To sustain a continuous sensory inference loop without inducing system-wide thermal throttling or latency degradation, rigorous architectural interventions are required. The systems engineering literature, heavily influenced by principles of neuromorphic computing, advocates for strict resource segregation. The system must artificially limit the capabilities of the sensory perimeter to preserve the integrity of the broader cognitive architecture.

### **CPU Core-Pinning and Scheduler Bypassing**

The default task scheduling algorithms in modern operating systems, such as the Linux Completely Fair Scheduler (CFS), are actively detrimental to continuous AI inference workloads. The CFS is designed to balance workloads across all available CPU cores, frequently migrating threads to distribute thermal loads evenly.7 However, in the context of memory-bound SLM inference, this thread migration is computationally disastrous. Moving an inference thread from one core to another necessitates a context switch, which includes flushing the Translation Lookaside Buffer (TLB) and evicting the L1 and L2 caches.9 When the thread resumes on a new core, the SLM's quantized weights must be re-fetched from the main memory, incurring massive latency penalties.9

The primary architectural remedy is aggressive CPU core-pinning, also known as CPU affinity.9 By utilizing system-level commands to explicitly bind the llama.cpp inference processes to a specific, isolated subset of CPU cores, engineers can enforce deterministic execution.9 This strategy yields significant benefits:

1. **Cache Warmth:** Pinned threads maintain spatial locality. The sub-tensors of the quantized SLM remain resident in the specific core's L2 cache, drastically reducing memory access latency and mitigating the jitter associated with cache misses.9
2. **Latency Stabilization:** By isolating the inference threads from normal OS scheduling interrupts, the variance in token generation is minimized, bringing worst-case latency deviations down to the microsecond level.7

Furthermore, in modern heterogeneous architectures featuring asymmetric core designs (e.g., platforms combining Performance-cores and Efficient-cores), execution frameworks can be configured to restrict the continuous sensory SLM entirely to the low-power E-cores.35 This deliberate resource segregation ensures that the P-cores remain entirely unpolluted and thermally optimal, held in reserve for high-priority, complex executive reasoning tasks triggered by anomalies detected in the sensory perimeter.

### **Metabolic Capping and Power Gating**

In biological neuroscience, memory capacity and synaptic activity are fundamentally restricted by the availability of metabolic energy. The concept of "metabolic capping" dictates that under limited energy resources, a neural network maximizes its performance through highly selective synaptic overgrowth followed by optimal, minimal-value deletion.36 Translated into silicon architecture, metabolic capping represents the deliberate imposition of hardware and algorithmic constraints to ensure sustainable, continuous operation over infinite time horizons.8

Given the thermal dynamics established previously, allowing a continuous inference loop to consume 100% of available CPU resources inevitably leads to thermal failure. To achieve a metabolic cap, systems engineers employ strict Thermal Design Power (TDP) management and leverage principles of "dark silicon"—the reality that not all transistors on a modern chip can be powered simultaneously without exceeding the thermal envelope.8

1. **Compute Throttling:** Rather than permitting the SLM inference to scale dynamically and crash into a thermal wall, engineers impose artificial ceilings on execution speed. For example, llama.cpp performance is optimized by deliberately setting the inference thread count lower than the total physical core count.3 Additionally, configuring the CPU governor to operate in a fixed, lowered power mode (e.g., capping a mobile chip at 15W rather than its 30W peak) ensures that the heat dissipation matches the heat generation, allowing the processor to reach a sustainable steady-state equilibrium.27
2. **Power Gating:** In tandem with compute throttling, modern hardware utilizes aggressive power gating to completely cut voltage to unpinned, idle CPU sectors.8 By artificially capping the metabolism of the sensory loop, the architecture sacrifices peak theoretical token generation speed in exchange for a flat, utterly predictable latency curve, which is an absolute requirement for an unbroken sensory perimeter.

## **Mapping Transient Entities to Dynamic Topological Graph Databases**

Once the sensory perimeter—functioning through the rapid statistical filtering of HTM poolers and the semantic extraction capabilities of quantized SLMs—identifies salient information within the unstructured byte stream, these transient entities must be integrated into a persistent memory architecture. Traditional relational databases, characterized by rigid schemas, are ill-equipped to handle the fluid, associative nature of sensory memory. Instead, modern architectures utilize dynamic topological graph databases to model the complex, evolving relationships between semantic nodes.10

### **SLMs as Translators to Topological Structures**

Recent studies indicate that heavily quantized, small-parameter models, despite their reduced capacity for complex, multi-hop generative reasoning, possess significant capability for structured graph-theoretic inference.39 Within the proposed sensory architecture, the SLM does not function as a conversational agent; rather, it acts as a deterministic translation filter. Prompt engineering techniques are utilized to constrain the SLM, forcing it to parse unstructured text inputs and output structured relational tuples (e.g., \[Entity A] -> ->).38

This methodology is foundational to frameworks such as Neural Graph Memory (NGM), wherein episodic experiences and continuous sensory streams are parsed by the language model and encoded as dynamic, sparse graphs.41 Within this topology, nodes represent multimodal embeddings or semantic concepts, while the connecting edges capture the temporal and semantic interactions extracted by the SLM. Because localized, CPU-bound SLMs generally struggle to maintain coherence over extended context windows, translating the text stream into a Cypher-queried graph database effectively offloads the burden of quantitative reasoning and long-term memory retrieval to the graph's native topological algorithms.10

### **Real-Time Hebbian Pruning and Strengthening**

To accurately mirror biological neuroplasticity and ensure the knowledge graph remains highly relevant over time, the topology of the database is not static; it evolves dynamically based on the frequency and context of the incoming sensory data.11 This evolution is managed through algorithmic implementations of Hebbian learning directly within the graph database structure.12

1. **Synapse Strengthening (Long-Term Potentiation):** When the SLM sensory filter repeatedly extracts a specific relationship between two entities from the unstructured data stream, the graph database increments the mathematical weight of the connecting edge.42 This represents a direct software analog to Hebbian learning: conceptual nodes that are frequently and concurrently activated by the sensory perimeter automatically strengthen their associative links, gradually elevating transient sensory observations into consolidated semantic knowledge.42
2. **Synaptic Pruning (Long-Term Depression):** Because the graph database operates on hardware subject to strict memory and metabolic capping, it cannot expand infinitely.44 To maintain querying efficiency, the architecture must enforce sparsity. Edges that are infrequently traversed naturally decay in weight over time.45 Furthermore, unsupervised topological reinforcement models iteratively prune network links that exhibit low utility or low topological overlap, mimicking biological synaptic depression.44 This dynamic process ensures the emergence of a "rich club" network topology, wherein a small number of densely connected, highly relevant nodes form an elite backbone for the rapid routing of associative memory.47

### **Academic Counter-Arguments to Hebbian Graph Architectures**

While the application of Hebbian principles to dynamic graph management provides a biologically plausible and computationally localized method for memory consolidation, the approach faces significant academic scrutiny and prominent counter-arguments within the machine learning community.

1. **Runaway Excitation and Lack of Homeostasis:** A pure Hebbian update rule is inherently unstable. Because edge weights increase continuously with co-activation, an unconstrained graph can suffer from runaway excitation, wherein a small cluster of nodes accrues infinite mathematical weight.13 Critics argue that without highly complex, non-linear homeostatic plasticity algorithms—such as Oja's rule, which normalizes the weight vectors, or the strict enforcement of Excitatory-Inhibitory (E-I) balance—Hebbian graphs quickly become dominated by hyper-connected hubs, destroying the sparse topology required for efficient querying.13
2. **Gradient Descent Superiority:** Prominent theorists assert that while localized Hebbian rules mimic biological constraints, they mathematically underperform global optimization strategies like Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) when attempting to minimize error across complex semantic clusters.50 Although recent studies have established a theoretical bridge indicating that gradient-based backpropagation (such as Dale's backpropagation) actually reinforces an overlapping subset of high-magnitude weights identical to those selected by Hebbian learning 14, SGD remains the preferred method for minimizing specific loss functions over entire graph structures.50
3. **Validation-Gated Consolidation vs. Reasoning Drift:** In a dynamic, unsupervised graph database, indiscriminately applying Hebbian strengthening to the outputs of an SLM risks severe "reasoning drift." If the quantized SLM occasionally hallucinates or misinterprets a sensory input, a pure Hebbian mechanism will stubbornly reinforce that hallucination upon subsequent repetitions. To counteract this, modern architectural proposals, such as the *Kairos* system, advocate for "validation-gated" Hebbian learning.45 In this alternative framework, structural adaptation and edge strengthening only occur if the extracted reasoning passes a multi-dimensional quality assessment governed by a separate, higher-order reasoning module, thereby preventing the permanent encoding of erroneous sensory data.45
