## **Executive Summary**

The architectural paradigm governing massive multi-agent distributed systems is currently undergoing a fundamental transition. Driven by the relentless demand for extreme concurrency, distributed environments scaling to hundreds of thousands or even millions of active nodes are increasingly abandoning centralized, persistent orchestration models. Traditional message-oriented middleware (MOM), characterized by centralized brokers and persistent event streaming logs, introduces inherent queuing delays, disk input/output (I/O) bottlenecks, and synchronous state locking. While highly effective for data pipelines and transaction processing, these traditional architectures catastrophically degrade when tasked with orchestrating high-frequency, massively concurrent agent models. In such environments, the arbitrary latency introduced by centralized buffering precipitates a systemic desynchronization analogous to "cognitive dissonance," wherein individual agents operate upon a delayed, fragmented representation of global reality, ultimately leading to divergent execution states and network collapse.

To resolve these severe architectural bottlenecks, contemporary distributed systems research and extreme-scale engineering implementations have increasingly adopted biologically-inspired communication models. These models rely heavily on zero-buffered, brokerless architectures and volatile, at-most-once publish/subscribe mechanisms. Libraries and runtimes such as ZeroMQ and NATS Core facilitate direct, peer-to-peer (P2P) state propagation with sub-millisecond latencies, closely mirroring the unbatched, highly parallel communication mechanisms found in the cellular networks of biological organisms. The academic and empirical consensus dictates that scaling an architecture to extreme concurrency—such as 500,000 to 5,000,000 active nodes—requires a specialized, dual-protocol approach. This involves utilizing highly targeted, deterministic connections for functional execution (analogous to synaptic cell-to-cell signaling) coupled inextricably with raw, at-most-once global broadcast networks for ambient, system-wide state awareness (analogous to endocrine chemical gradients).

However, the realization of this decentralized, highly concurrent actor model—most prominently engineered within the Erlang Virtual Machine (BEAM) and its Elixir ecosystem—introduces a severe engineering limitation: the "broadcast storm." Standard distributed topologies inherently rely on fully connected $O(n^2)$ network meshes and strictly synchronized global namespaces. When subjected to the unbuffered, volatile communication patterns of a bio-inspired network, these global registries violently degrade into cascading network meltdowns, consuming all available computational resources through redundant message propagation. Mitigating these storms without reverting to centralized batching or buffering mandates advanced, decentralized topological partitioning. Research and production environments have successfully solved this through frameworks such as Scalable Distributed (SD) Erlang’s s\_groups, configurable partial-view overlays like the Partisan runtime, and application-layer edge-fan-out algorithms.

This comprehensive report provides an exhaustive synthesis of the academic literature, empirical performance data, and theoretical frameworks surrounding zero-buffered communication. It evaluates the physical validity of unbatched state propagation, the performance limits of brokerless topologies versus centralized systems, the utilization of ambient global transmissions, and the intricate engineering required to mitigate broadcast storms within massive-scale actor models.

## **The Zero-Buffering Physical Mandate**

### **The Phenomenon of Cognitive Dissonance and Delayed Reality**

In the study of complex adaptive systems and multi-agent organism models, the "zero-buffering mandate" posits that immediate, unbatched state propagation is not merely an optimization, but a fundamental physical necessity required to prevent structural and logical divergence.1 The theoretical underpinning of this mandate lies in the concept of temporal accuracy versus data durability. When a distributed architecture relies on centralized, buffered queues, information regarding global state mutations is artificially delayed to guarantee its safe persistence. As independent agents compute their subsequent actions based on this delayed information, a measurable temporal discrepancy ($\\Delta t$) emerges between the true, absolute global state and the agent's internal, localized representation of that state.3

In socio-cognitive modeling, psychological frameworks, and biological predictive processing, this sustained discrepancy is formalized as "cognitive dissonance" or "prediction error".1 In a technological context, cognitive dissonance occurs when a multi-agent system (MAS) operates on a temporally delayed reality.4 If the system is engineered to simulate a reactive biological organism or a swarm intelligence, any delay between the moment a state-changing decision is executed and the moment that state is globally propagated leads to cascading behavioral errors.3 Buffering mechanisms inherent in centralized message brokers are structurally designed to prioritize total data durability over temporal immediacy; by pre-fetching, batching, and writing message streams to non-volatile disk storage, traditional brokers ensure zero data loss, but guarantee that the data delivered represents a historical artifact rather than the absolute present.

Recent literature modeling active inference and predictive coding in multi-agent environments highlights that agents continuously attempt to minimize surprise (prediction error) through interaction with their local environment.5 If an agent is fed a buffered, delayed stream of persistent events from a central broker, it will act upon stale stimuli. This introduces "social hysteresis" into the network—a phenomenon where the system's collective response to an external stimulus is fatally delayed and corrupted by its adherence to an outdated emergent collective memory.2 Therefore, mitigating cognitive dissonance in a distributed organism model requires eliminating the buffer entirely, forcing agents to process the absolute present, even at the cost of dropping historical messages.6

### **Empirical Validation of Unbatched State Propagation**

Established theoretical frameworks in bio-inspired computing and telecommunications demonstrate that zero-latency, or near-zero buffering, is strictly required for the operational stability of decentralized, highly concurrent systems.7 Models that leverage zero-buffering mechanisms—such as direct memory access transfers or immediate, unbatched physical data propagation—closely mirror the instantaneous, highly interdependent signaling pathways required in neural networks and continuous cellular processes.8

For example, biological processes such as cellular sorting, the refinement of chemical components, and the propagation of electrical potentials along the archaic vegetative systems of complex organisms are entirely continuous and unbuffered.8 Employees or agents performing tasks at various interconnected nodes in an unbuffered continuous sequence are directly and immediately affected by the actions of neighboring agents, necessitating continuous, localized liaison without the intervention of a centralized governing memory buffer.8

Research into the graph-theoretical topologies of multi-agent systems further validates this mandate. Traditional computational graph models rely on static, centralized algorithms requiring a global view of the network.12 In contrast, a biologically-inspired MAS equips each agent with adaptive, autonomous capabilities utilizing localized, unbuffered information to dynamically form and modify operational paths.12 This decentralized approach shifts the burden of memory storage and state awareness away from a centralized queue, embedding it directly into the real-time topological distribution of the agent network.12 By enforcing unbatched state propagation, communication entropy and specialization efficiency are optimized, validating the necessity of zero-buffering in avoiding delayed reality and preserving the immediate, reactive nature of the swarm.13

## **ZeroMQ & Brokerless Topologies**

### **The Architectural Divergence of Peer-to-Peer vs. Centralized Brokers**

To satisfy the rigorous demands of the zero-buffering physical mandate, system architects must utilize messaging paradigms that completely bypass the traditional "hub-and-spoke" architecture of centralized brokers. ZeroMQ (0MQ) stands as a foundational library for this class of distributed engineering. Rather than operating as a standalone server, ZeroMQ functions as an embedded socket library that routes messages directly between concurrent endpoints using localized, application-defined routing logic, entirely eliminating the centralized middleman.14

This brokerless, peer-to-peer (P2P) topology resolves the fundamental architectural drawbacks inherent to centralized broker models. In a traditional brokered system, every application must connect to a central node, meaning no two edge components communicate directly. This requires an excessive volume of network round-trips, inherently increasing latency and exposing the broker as a single point of failure and a massive performance bottleneck.15 Under extreme loads, the broker component reaches 100% CPU and memory utilization, while the distributed edge nodes remain idle waiting for message acknowledgments.15 By embedding the routing mechanisms directly into the agent processes, ZeroMQ enables independent scaling, where the throughput capacity of the network is constrained only by the aggregate bandwidth of the underlying physical fabric, rather than the processing limits of a single central authority.14

### **Academic Data on Extreme-Scale P2P Performance**

Empirical performance benchmarks and academic evaluations consistently highlight a massive discrepancy in throughput, latency, and hardware utilization between brokerless socket libraries and centralized message brokers when subjected to high-frequency workloads.18

While robust, centralized event-streaming platforms like Apache Kafka achieve exceptional throughput—capable of processing up to 605 Megabytes per second (MB/s) or roughly 150,000 to 600,000 messages per second under optimal conditions—they do so at the strict expense of latency.14 Kafka achieves its high throughput through aggressive batching, compression, and persistent writes to an append-only disk log.14 This mechanical reality results in base latencies ranging between 10 milliseconds (ms) and 50 ms, which can aggressively spike to 100 ms or more during OS cache misses.22 Traditional AMQP brokers like RabbitMQ, which also rely on centralized memory and disk persistence, typically bottleneck much earlier, demonstrating a peak throughput around 38,000 to 43,000 messages per second, with latencies degrading significantly as message volumes increase.14

In stark contrast, ZeroMQ leverages kernel-bypass asynchronous I/O, strict in-memory handling, and localized message batching to achieve performance metrics that are orders of magnitude faster.24 Because it removes the network hop to a broker and executes entirely in volatile memory, ZeroMQ achieves throughputs easily exceeding 300,000 messages per second on standard hardware, with message latency measured consistently in the sub-millisecond to microsecond ($\\mu s$) range.14 For context, benchmark evaluations indicate that ZeroMQ can process a batch of 10,000 localized messages in approximately 15 milliseconds—a volume that requires a full second to traverse a centralized RabbitMQ cluster.14

## **Ambient Global Transmissions**

### **At-Most-Once vs. Persistent Messaging for State Signals**

In the study of biological organisms, the propagation of global physiological state is rarely managed through persistent, guaranteed, or ordered delivery channels. Instead, organic systems rely heavily on "ambient" signaling models, most prominently achieved through the diffusion of endocrine hormones and the establishment of chemical gradients.11 When a biological cell generates and secretes a hormone, it acts as a decentralized publisher. It blindly broadcasts the chemical event to its immediate neighbors and the surrounding matrix.27 The cell does not maintain a ledger of subscriber cells, it does not guarantee that specific distant tissues will receive the molecule, and it certainly does not wait for an acknowledgment of receipt. This biological routing is strictly an "at-most-once" delivery mechanism; if a receptor cell is inactive or the molecule degrades, the receiving tissue simply relies on the subsequent ambient concentration of the ongoing chemical gradient.27

Translating this ambient biological model into distributed computing architecture necessitates abandoning at-least-once or exactly-once message delivery protocols when broadcasting global state. Messaging infrastructures like NATS Core are engineered precisely for this ambient broadcast paradigm. Unlike its persistence-oriented counterpart (JetStream) or systems like Kafka, NATS Core operates as a highly volatile, strictly in-memory publish-subscribe engine.25 Written in Go, NATS Core achieves an astonishing throughput of 8 to 11 million messages per second by entirely eschewing data persistence.25 It employs a pure "fire-and-forget" model, equivalent to Quality of Service (QoS) level 0 (at-most-once delivery).25

In QoS 0 environments, messages are delivered without requiring any acknowledgment from the receiving client, representing the fastest protocol with the absolute lowest computational overhead.31 If a subscribing node in the system goes offline, any messages broadcasted during its downtime are permanently lost to that node.25 Within the context of a massively concurrent, biologically-inspired multi-agent model, this volatility is a critical engineering requirement. Replaying thousands of stale, historical state signals to an agent that has just recovered from a crash would immediately induce the aforementioned cognitive dissonance, forcing the agent to compute actions based on a past environment rather than the absolute present.2 The agent only requires the current ambient state—the present concentration of the computational "chemical gradient." Therefore, persistent logs are not merely unnecessary for real-time state synchronization; they are structurally detrimental to the organism's cohesion.

### **The Dual-Protocol Approach: Deterministic vs. Ambient Signaling**

Because no single protocol can satisfy the demands of both high-fidelity targeted execution and low-overhead global broadcasting, leading literature and architectural designs in distributed systems advocate for a dual-protocol approach. This paradigm deliberately splits communication into two distinct biological analogues: cell-to-cell deterministic signaling and ambient chemical gradients.27

#### **1. Cell-to-Cell Execution (Deterministic Networking)**

Analogous to the highly targeted, high-speed synapses of the biological nervous system, computational commands that mutate immediate local state, initiate physical transactions, or transfer direct resources require deterministic protocols.11 In distributed computing, these localized interactions are handled via the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP/IP), direct Inter-Process Communication (IPC), or tightly bound Remote Procedure Calls (gRPC).36 These connections operate on synchronous or strictly ordered asynchronous channels.37 They guarantee packet delivery, enforce strict ordering, and require explicit acknowledgment (ACK) to ensure that critical, non-idempotent operations—such as the digital equivalent of cellular division or direct resource transfer—are executed flawlessly without duplication or loss.36

#### **2. Chemical Gradients (Ambient Publish/Subscribe)**

Conversely, non-critical, system-wide state changes—such as fluctuations in system load, the detection of external threats, or shifts in operational phases—must be broadcasted amorphously without crippling the network. Analogous to the endocrine system, these global signals are distributed as "chemical gradients".11 Libraries like ZeroMQ (utilizing the PUB/SUB pattern) or NATS Core act as the computational interstitial matrix.11 In this paradigm, an agent navigating the distributed system determines its actions not by requesting specific coordinates from a central database, but by sensing the varying "concentration" or frequency of incoming ambient messages. By evaluating the density of specific message topics, an agent can autonomously determine its spatial or logical proximity to an event and migrate toward or away from the source (analogous to bacterial chemotaxis).40

#### **Digital Hormone Models (DHM)**

The viability of this dual-protocol approach is heavily substantiated by research into Digital Hormone Models (DHM) for self-organizing robotics and autonomous swarms.42 Originating from Turing's 1952 reaction-diffusion equations, the DHM simulates multi-agent systems where individual computational cells secrete digital "hormones" (activators and inhibitors) that diffuse across network grids.42 The diffusion of these unbuffered messages creates localized gradients.42 Based on the varying concentration of these hormonal messages, coupled with local internal state variables and direct physical sensor data (the deterministic layer), individual agents autonomously select and execute actions such as migration, structural differentiation, and network reconfiguration.42 Because the agents rely on volatile, localized hormone diffusion rather than a globally synchronized memory ledger, the swarm can dynamically route around failures, self-heal, and coordinate complex metamorphic actions—such as splitting and merging—without any centralized intervention.42

## **The Engineering Reality: The Broadcast Storm**

### **The Erlang Actor Model and Extreme Concurrency Limits**

While the theoretical models of zero-buffered, bio-inspired networking are sound, applying these concepts to practical, massively concurrent computing environments exposes severe engineering limitations. The implementation of such models is most naturally suited to the Actor paradigm of concurrency, which is dominantly realized by the Erlang Virtual Machine (BEAM) and its modern dialect, Elixir.44

The Actor model encapsulates state within millions of isolated, independent processes (actors) that communicate exclusively via asynchronous message passing.46 Because each actor strictly isolates its own memory and execution context, a failure in one process does not corrupt the overarching system, perfectly embodying the "let it crash" fault-tolerance philosophy necessary for biological simulation.47 The BEAM environment is exquisitely optimized for this, enabling a single physical machine to spawn and manage millions of lightweight, concurrent processes.46

However, scaling the Erlang VM horizontally across distributed hardware to support 500,000 or more concurrent nodes introduces a catastrophic architectural bottleneck.50 Distributed Erlang was conceptually designed to support reliable clusters, but it natively assumes a fully connected mesh network topology.50 In a full-mesh configuration, every Erlang node in the cluster must maintain an active, bidirectional TCP/IP connection with every other node.50 Mathematically, a cluster of $n$ nodes requires $O(n^2)$ connections.50 Consequently, as the cluster scales beyond 40 to 60 physical nodes, the sheer volume of background network traffic—consisting of periodic heartbeat signals (net\_tick\_time) to verify node vitality and the continuous synchronization of the global process namespace—overwhelms the network interfaces and consumes all available CPU cycles.49

When massive volumes of unbuffered, zero-latency messages are rapidly broadcast across this fragile, fully connected mesh, the system experiences a "broadcast storm".53 A broadcast storm is a cascading network meltdown where legitimate functional data is drowned out by exponential packet collisions, redundant retransmissions, and the relentless synchronization of global state registries.54 As the network saturates, heartbeats are dropped, nodes mistakenly declare their peers "dead," and the system initiates massive, cluster-wide topology rebuilds, resulting in total paralysis.52

### **Topological Mitigation: SD Erlang and s\_groups**

To preserve the zero-buffering mandate while preventing network meltdowns, systems researchers have engineered advanced topological structures that explicitly dismantle the fully connected mesh. One of the most prominent academic solutions is Scalable Distributed (SD) Erlang, developed in conjunction with the European RELEASE project.50

SD Erlang resolves the $O(n^2)$ connection overhead by partitioning the global Erlang network into localized, overlapping scalability groups called s\_groups.50 Within an individual s\_group, nodes maintain the traditional transitive, full-mesh connectivity required for high-speed local actor supervision.50 However, connections to nodes residing outside the specific s\_group are strictly non-transitive; inter-group communication is routed exclusively through explicitly designated "gateway nodes" using pairwise connections.50

Crucially, SD Erlang eliminates the requirement for a monolithic, globally synchronized process namespace.50 Actors register their names and states locally within the boundaries of their respective s\_groups.50 By compartmentalizing the namespace, the volume of global synchronization traffic generated during a node failure or process creation is drastically reduced.50 Extensive evaluations of SD Erlang utilized specialized "Chaos Monkey" tools (specifically the rhesus termination module) to randomly terminate nodes across systems scaling up to 256 physical hosts and 6,144 cores.50 Operating complex benchmarks—including Ant Colony Optimization (ACO), distributed Orbit calculations, and a 150,000-line Sim-Diasca discrete simulation engine—SD Erlang successfully prevented broadcast storms.50 The research proved that by partitioning recovery data and network topology, systems could scale by orders of magnitude while fully preserving the distributed Erlang reliability model.50

### **Configurable Overlays and the Partisan Runtime**

Pushing beyond the limitations of strict grouping, contemporary distributed systems research introduced Partisan, an alternative distribution layer for Erlang that entirely abstracts the underlying network topology, allowing developers to configure custom connection overlays at runtime.57

Partisan recognizes that the rigid full-mesh topology of Distributed Erlang is fundamentally incompatible with the high churn and massive scale of biological or peer-to-peer modeling.52 Instead of forcing a one-size-fits-all mesh, Partisan enables developers to deploy varying architectures—including client-server, publish-subscribe, and highly advanced peer-to-peer overlays—without altering the underlying application semantics.58 For extreme-scale environments, Partisan utilizes a modified implementation of the HyParView (Hybrid Partial View) membership protocol.52

HyParView operates by maintaining a small, active list of active peer connections alongside a larger, passive list of backup nodes, creating a decentralized gossip network.52 By utilizing this partial-view topology, no single node is required to track the status of the entire 500,000-node cluster, effectively neutralizing the exponential connection overhead and eliminating the structural prerequisites of a broadcast storm. Benchmarks of the Partisan runtime demonstrated spectacular performance gains over standard Distributed Erlang: under high concurrency and network congestion, Partisan achieved up to a 38.07x increase in raw throughput and a 13.5x reduction in message latency.57

### **Cache-Locking Mitigation and Edge Fan-Out: The Discord Case Study**

Empirical data from commercial deployments operating at the absolute limits of concurrency provides practical blueprints for mitigating broadcast storms at the application layer. The engineering architecture of Discord, an application relying on Elixir and the Erlang VM to support 5,000,000 concurrent users per single cluster node, offers definitive insights into these mechanisms.59

Discord's infrastructure operates on a massive Pub/Sub model, where individual user sessions (Actor processes) communicate with distributed Guild processes. When a highly active guild (e.g., containing 30,000 concurrent users) experienced an event, the central actor attempted to fan-out that message to all 30,000 individual session actors.59 Because standard Erlang message passing (send/2) forcefully de-schedules the calling process to ensure fairness, an individual message dispatch consumed between 30 $\\mu s$ and 70 $\\mu s$.59 A single broadcast to a massive guild resulted in 2.1 seconds of pure CPU time simply to queue the messages, creating a localized broadcast storm that starved the node of resources.59 To resolve this without resorting to external, centralized batching mechanisms, Discord engineers developed Manifold, a decentralized distribution library.59 Manifold shifts the computational burden of the fan-out to the edge of the network. Instead of the origin node dispatching 30,000 discrete messages across the cluster, it calculates the physical location of the recipients and transmits exactly one network message to each participating remote host.59 A localized partitioner on the receiving node then accepts the single message and executes the final, high-speed delivery to the local Process IDs (PIDs).59

Furthermore, Discord encountered severe "cache-locking" and recovery latency during network partitions.59 The application utilized a consistent hashing ring, stored in an Erlang Term Storage (ETS) table (a mutable C dictionary), to look up the locations of decentralized actors.59 While ETS lookups were fast (7 $\\mu s$), the Erlang garbage collection architecture requires that data retrieved from an ETS table be physically copied into the requesting actor's isolated memory heap.59 When a node crashed and 500,000 live sessions simultaneously reconnected, this memory-copying requirement paralyzed the VM, resulting in a 17.5-second recovery delay that triggered cascading timeouts and further storms across the system.59 To eliminate this bottleneck, the engineering team implemented FastGlobal, a library that bypasses ETS entirely. By dynamically compiling the consistent hash ring directly into a BEAM VM module, the data is placed into a read-only shared heap accessible by all actors without memory-copying overhead.59 This architectural pivot reduced lookup costs to 0.3 $\\mu s$ and plummeted the global recovery time to a mere 750 milliseconds, permanently stabilizing the multi-agent network under catastrophic load.59
